Columbia affiliates donated far more to Biden, but Trump fares better than in 2016

By Luke Dobrovic and Ellen Guo Edited by Raeedah Wahid and Jessica Li

November 6, 2020

As 2020 election results roll in from key states nationwide, the national anticipation for a final decision serves as a stark reminder that this year’s race is a pivotal moment in political history. Like many across the country, Columbia affiliates have invested in this election—both with their time and their checkbooks.

While Democratic presidential candidate Joe Biden received more money than President Donald Trump from Columbia donors this year, affiliates had preferred former Secretary of State Hillary Clinton in 2016 to Trump by a much wider margin. Clinton received $741 from Columbia affiliates for every Trump dollar, as opposed to a 34-1 ratio between Biden and Trump. Current Federal Election Commission data does not yet include donations from the final, most intense three weeks of the campaign, which usually garner greater donations—as seen in 2016 with over 12 percent of donations contributed in those final weeks.

The nationwide energy behind this election, especially among Columbia affiliates, who self-identify the University as their employer on campaign contributions, can be traced through these campaign donations. Even amid a pandemic, the number of individual contributions from Columbia-affiliated donors has increased 93 percent since 2016, indicating a greater popular and financial interest within the Columbia community.

Part of the apparent rise in engagement with presidential campaigns can be attributed to the saturation of the democratic primaries. Donations to presidential candidates in 2020 largely surpassed donations to Senate and Congressional races, which occupied six of the top 15 funds in 2016 but only two in 2020. The five presidential campaigns were all Democratic candidates: Joe Biden, Elizabeth Warren, Bernie Sanders, Cory Booker, and Kamala Harris. Notably, donations to Sanders, the only candidate to run in both races, remained at around $70,000 from 2016 to 2020, indicating a solid base of support among Columbia donors for the progressive wing of the Democratic party.

Biden outraises Trump 34 to 1

Throughout the campaign cycle, Biden has consistently outraised Trump. Current data shows that Columbia affiliates contributed 34 times more money overall and over 10 times more individual donations to the former vice president. The average donation to Biden is also nearly three times the value of that to Trump.

However, since then, Biden has led fundraising by a large margin, especially as he began to secure delegates and win primaries in spring 2020. Biden reached his fundraising peak from Columbia donors, $13,700, on August 18, during the Democratic National Convention when he was officially declared the Democratic nominee. Thereafter, donations to Biden took off, securing his position as the primary recipient of presidential campaign donations from Columbia affiliates, while daily donations to Trump failed to break the low hundreds.

Columbia donors only gave $9,371 in total to Trump in 2020, but this low figure is still an almost-14-fold increase over the $676 they gave him in 2016. Much of this growth can be attributed to the fact that Columbia affiliates gave much more to Trump’s competitors in 2016, whereas this year, Trump was the assumed Republican candidate by virtue of his role as the incumbent. In the 2016 elections, Trump barely outraised even George Pataki, who dropped out of the race in December 2015. In fact, the first Trump donation from a Columbia affiliate that cycle was recorded in March 2016, once he began to secure his position as the presumptive Republican nominee following his first Super Tuesday primary victory.

The proportionally greater support for Clinton’s campaign against Trump in comparison to that of Biden can partly be attributed to the larger role of ActBlue in 2020, a democratic political action committee and super PAC hybrid that can both donate to individual candidates and make independent expenditures. Super PACs are political action committees that can take unlimited contributions but may not coordinate with campaigns.

The ActBlue committee became the primary destination for Columbia donations in 2020, above even the Biden campaign itself. It raised $744,000 for the 2020 elections, over double the amount it raised in 2016. This may be emblematic of how the Democratic Party has reworked donation strategies to direct funds toward national leaders instead of individual candidates: The Biden campaign website itself provides a link to donate through ActBlue instead of to its own Biden For President committee directly.

Across all Columbia affiliates, except undergraduate students, donation counts increased from 2016 to 2020. Administrators, graduate students, and non-faculty teachers made over twice as many individual donations this election than in 2016. Donations from undergraduate students dropped 8 percent between elections, perhaps due to the economic impact of the COVID-19 pandemic on the youngest and least financially-secure demographic of the Columbia population. Additionally, Columbia undergraduate students who donate may not choose to self-report themselves as “employees” of the University, so FEC data may not show the full extent of campaign contributions from the undergraduate student body.

In what many have called the “most important election of our lifetime,” Columbia-affiliated donors have contributed millions of dollars to candidates and political groups at all levels. After results have finished rolling in, Columbia community members will see if the millions they have donated have had the desired impact.

About the numbers

Contribution data is collected from the Federal Election Commission. FEC data reported only represents donations until October 12, 2020. More recent data was not available at the time of publication. Data for the 2016 cycle included any donations reported from January 1, 2015, to December 31, 2016.

Data was scraped and cleaned to include only those donations given by self-identified employees of Columbia. The number of donors was processed to be unique, but the number of donations is not, as a single donor may have made multiple donations to any number of campaigns.

Occupational data was manually sorted into the categories shown. “Faculty” includes adjunct, assistant, and tenure-track professors and deans. “Teaching positions” include lecturers, educators, teachers, instructors, and coaches. “Admin” refers to administrative managers, assistants, directors, and executives in business, communications, media, finance, IT, operations, development, HR, and marketing roles. “Academia and research” refers to historians, chemists, librarians, and postdoctoral researchers. “Medical & health” includes physicians, public health experts, and medical researchers. “Undergraduates” also refers to unspecified interns and work-study recipients. “Graduate students” include doctoral and masters students as well as unspecified teaching assistants.
